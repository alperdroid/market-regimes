\begin{abstract}
\noindent
We compare three families of market-regime classifiers---a rule-based volatility
trigger, a Gaussian Hidden Markov Model (HMM), and a decoupled machine-learning
pipeline (Gaussian Mixture clustering followed by regime-specialist Random Forest
return forecasters)---and ask whether conditioning a US sector-rotation strategy on the
inferred regime improves out-of-sample, after-cost performance relative to a passive
S\&P~500 benchmark. Using daily data on nine SPDR sector ETFs from 2004 to 2025, all
regime models are estimated in a strict expanding-window walk-forward design, and
portfolios are formed each month by regime-conditional minimum-variance (MVP) and
maximum-Sharpe tangency (TPF) optimisation with Ledoit--Wolf covariance shrinkage. We
find that regime conditioning delivers a large and robust reduction in volatility and
drawdown---the minimum-variance strategies cut the maximum drawdown from roughly
$-60\%$ (S\&P~500) to $-37\%$ while modestly raising the Sharpe ratio---but that the
apparent \emph{return} advantage of the best machine-learning strategy does not survive
a data-snooping correction (White's Reality Check, $p=0.56$), even though its
risk-adjusted Sharpe ratio does (Deflated Sharpe Ratio $=0.98$). We conclude that the
economic value of regime-based tactical allocation in this setting is concentrated in
risk control rather than in abnormal returns, and that high-turnover return-timing
variants are largely competed away by transaction costs.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
% =====================================================================
A long tradition in empirical finance holds that asset returns are generated by a small
number of recurring ``states of the world''---calm bull markets, turbulent transitions,
and crises---with sharply different first and second moments \citep{hamilton1989,
ang2002, guidolin2007}. If such regimes are identifiable in real time, an investor could
in principle rotate across assets to harvest the favourable states and de-risk in the
unfavourable ones. This paper studies that proposition for US equity \emph{sectors}, the
most liquid vehicle for expressing tactical macro views, and confronts a practical
question that much of the academic literature elides: once a regime model is estimated
only on information available at the time of trading, and once realistic transaction
costs are charged, does regime conditioning actually beat a passive benchmark?

\paragraph{Objective and research question.}
The objective of this paper is to quantify, on a strictly out-of-sample and after-cost
basis, the economic value added by market-regime conditioning in US sector allocation,
and to decompose that value into a \emph{risk} component and a \emph{return} component.
We address three research questions. (RQ1) Does a data-driven latent-state model (HMM or
machine learning) identify regimes more usefully than a transparent VIX rule? (RQ2) Is
any incremental value attributable to regime \emph{persistence}, isolated by contrasting
the Markovian HMM with a memoryless mixture? (RQ3) Does the best-performing strategy
survive a formal data-snooping correction, or is its outperformance a selection artefact?

\paragraph{Contributions.}
We make three contributions. First, we place three structurally distinct regime
classifiers on a common, strictly out-of-sample footing: a transparent VIX threshold
rule, a Gaussian HMM that explicitly models regime \emph{persistence}, and a two-stage
machine-learning pipeline that separates regime \emph{identification} (unsupervised
clustering) from regime-conditional \emph{return forecasting} (supervised ensembles). The
VIX-versus-HMM contrast isolates the value of a data-driven latent-state model over a
fixed rule, while the HMM-versus-ML contrast isolates the value of modelling temporal
dependence (the HMM's Markov transition matrix) versus a memoryless mixture. Second, we
evaluate each regime signal through two portfolio lenses---minimum-variance (which
depends only on the covariance matrix) and maximum-Sharpe tangency (which additionally
depends on expected returns)---so as to separate the value of risk estimation from the
value of return forecasting. Third, and most importantly for the credibility of the
exercise, we measure portfolio turnover directly, charge transaction costs, and subject
the leading strategy to formal multiple-testing corrections, because evaluating ten
strategies on one sample mechanically inflates the best in-sample result
\citep{harvey2016}.

Our headline finding is a clean dissociation. Regime conditioning reliably \emph{reduces
risk}: across all three classifiers, the minimum-variance variants achieve Sharpe ratios
of $0.46$--$0.50$ against the S\&P~500's $0.44$ at roughly two-thirds of the volatility
and three-fifths of the drawdown, with low turnover that is robust to plausible trading
costs. But the one strategy that appears to \emph{add return}---the machine-learning
tangency portfolio, with a $9.5\%$ annualised return and a $4.3\%$ CAPM alpha---trades
more than six times its capital per year, and its return advantage over the S\&P~500 is
statistically indistinguishable from chance once data snooping is accounted for. The
value of ``intelligence'' in regime modelling, in this dataset, is defensive.

\paragraph{Outline.}
The remainder proceeds as follows. Section~\ref{sec:lit} reviews the relevant literature
on regime-switching models, volatility-based classification, machine learning for

Section~\ref{sec:concl} concludes.

\section{Related Literature}\label{sec:lit}
% =====================================================================
\paragraph{Regime-switching models.}
The Markov-switching framework of \citet{hamilton1989} formalised the idea that financial
time series alternate between discrete states with state-dependent dynamics.
\citet{ang2002} and \citet{guidolin2007} applied multivariate regime-switching models to
equity allocation and documented that correlations and volatilities rise sharply in
``bear'' regimes, eroding diversification precisely when it is most needed. The Gaussian
HMM we employ is the canonical estimation device for such latent-state models, fitted by
the Baum--Welch expectation--maximisation algorithm \citep{baum1970, dempster1977} and
decoded by the \citet{viterbi1967} algorithm.

\paragraph{Volatility as a regime proxy.}
The CBOE VIX index, the risk-neutral expected volatility of the S\&P~500 over the next 30
days \citep{whaley2009}, is the most widely used real-time stress gauge. Practitioner and
academic conventions treat VIX below 20 as calm, 20--30 as elevated, and above 30 as
crisis \citep{whaley2009, bloom2009}, a mapping we adopt as our transparent benchmark
classifier. \citet{bloom2009} shows that volatility shocks are themselves a driver of
real and financial dynamics, motivating the use of the \emph{change} in VIX as a feature.

\paragraph{Machine learning for regimes and returns.}
Gaussian Mixture Models provide a soft-clustering analogue to the HMM without the Markov
persistence assumption \citep{mclachlan2000}. Random Forests \citep{breiman2001} are a
standard non-linear ensemble for return and risk prediction \citep{gu2020}, valued for
their robustness to noise and interpretable feature importances. Our pipeline
deliberately \emph{decouples} clustering from forecasting, training one specialist forest
per regime.

\paragraph{Portfolio construction and estimation risk.}
\citet{markowitz1952} mean--variance optimisation is notoriously sensitive to estimation
error in expected returns, to the point that the na\"ive $1/N$ portfolio is hard to beat
out of sample \citep{demiguel2009}. Two responses inform our design: minimum-variance
investing, which avoids the expected-return input entirely and has delivered strong
risk-adjusted performance empirically \citep{clarke2006}, and covariance shrinkage, for
which \citet{ledoit2004} derive an optimal linear combination of the sample covariance
and a structured target.

\paragraph{Performance evaluation under data snooping.}
Because we search over many strategies, in-sample outperformance is upward biased.
\citet{white2000} introduced the Reality Check, a bootstrap test of whether the
\emph{best} of many strategies beats a benchmark, implemented here with the stationary
bootstrap of \citet{politis1994}. \citet{bailey2014} propose the Deflated Sharpe Ratio,
which benchmarks a strategy's Sharpe against the expected maximum Sharpe attainable by
chance across the trials searched. \citet{harvey2016} make the general case for raising
significance thresholds in light of the multiplicity of tested strategies.

\section{Data}\label{sec:data}
% =====================================================================
The sample comprises daily data from January 2004 to December 2025 (5{,}533 trading
days), sourced from Yahoo!\ Finance and the Federal Reserve Economic Database (FRED) and
cached locally for reproducibility.

\paragraph{Asset universe.}
The nine SPDR Select Sector ETFs---Technology (XLK), Consumer Discretionary (XLY),
Financials (XLF), Industrials (XLI), Materials (XLB), Energy (XLE), Consumer Staples
(XLP), Health Care (XLV), and Utilities (XLU)---span the investable US equity market,
with SPDR S\&P~500 (SPY) as the benchmark. We use dividend- and split-adjusted closing
prices.

\paragraph{State and macro variables.}
The CBOE VIX index proxies market stress. The risk-free rate is the 13-week
Treasury-bill annualised yield (\texttt{\textasciicircum IRX}), converted to a daily
decimal rate. Two macro--financial conditioning variables are drawn from FRED: the TED
spread (\texttt{TEDRATE}), a funding-stress indicator, and the 10-year--2-year Treasury
term spread (\texttt{T10Y2Y}), a leading indicator of the business cycle. Log returns and
excess returns are defined as
\begin{equation}
  r_{i,t}=\ln\!\left(\frac{P_{i,t}}{P_{i,t-1}}\right),
  \qquad
  e_{i,t}=r_{i,t}-\rf_t,
\end{equation}
where $e_{i,t}$ is the excess return over the risk-free rate.

\section{Methodology}\label{sec:method}
% =====================================================================

\subsection{Feature construction}
Two feature sets are built. The \emph{regime} features used by the HMM and the Gaussian
Mixture are the daily change in VIX, $\dvix_t=\mathrm{VIX}_t-\mathrm{VIX}_{t-1}$, stacked
with the nine sector log returns (a 10-dimensional vector). The \emph{forecasting}
features used by the Random Forests are richer: the VIX level, $\dvix$, the TED spread,
the term spread, and sector log returns lagged $1,2,3,5$ days. This separation encodes an
economic prior---\emph{which} regime prevails is driven primarily by volatility, whereas
\emph{what returns to expect within} a regime depends on a broader macro and momentum
information set.

\subsection{Regime identification}

\paragraph{(a) VIX rule.}
The transparent benchmark assigns each day to Calm ($\mathrm{VIX}<20$), Transitional
($20\le\mathrm{VIX}<30$), or Crisis ($\mathrm{VIX}\ge30$), following \citet{whaley2009}
and \citet{bloom2009}.

\paragraph{(b) Hidden Markov Model.}
We fit a three-state Gaussian HMM in which the observed feature vector is, conditional on
the latent state $s_t\in\{0,1,2\}$, multivariate normal,
\begin{equation}
  x_t \mid s_t=k \;\sim\; \mathcal{N}(\mu_k,\Sigma_k),
  \qquad
  A_{ij}=\Pr(s_{t+1}=j\mid s_t=i),
\end{equation}
with full state-specific covariances $\Sigma_k$ and a first-order Markov transition
matrix $A$. Parameters are estimated by Baum--Welch EM; because EM converges only to
local optima, we take the best of twenty random restarts by log-likelihood, and recover
the most probable state path by Viterbi decoding. HMM state indices are not identified by
the likelihood (the ``label-switching'' problem), so we sort the states by their
state-conditional mean $\dvix$, fixing state $0$ as Calm and state $2$ as Crisis and
permuting $A$, $\mu_k$ and $\Sigma_k$ accordingly.

\paragraph{(c) Machine-learning pipeline.}
The ML arm decouples identification from forecasting. \emph{Step 1} fits a
three-component Gaussian Mixture on the regime features and assigns each day a hard
cluster label, sorted by $\dvix$ for identifiability; unlike the HMM, the mixture is
memoryless. \emph{Step 2} trains three independent multi-output Random Forest
regressors---one per regime---each fitted only on the days the mixture assigned to that
regime and each predicting the next day's nine sector returns from the forecasting
features (300 trees, depth 5, minimum leaf size 20). At prediction time the mixture
selects the regime and the corresponding forest produces the return forecast that feeds
the optimiser.

\paragraph{(d) Walk-forward estimation.}
All learned models are fitted in an \emph{expanding-window walk-forward}: trained on all
data up to time $t$, used to label/forecast the subsequent year, then refitted with the
new year appended. We require a 504-day (two-year) minimum training window and refit
every 252 days. This guarantees that all regime labels and return forecasts used in the
backtest are strictly out-of-sample---the single most important safeguard against
look-ahead bias.

\subsection{Regime-conditional CAPM}
To characterise the economic content of the regimes, we estimate, for each sector $i$ and
each VIX-defined regime $s$, the conditional Capital Asset Pricing Model (CAPM) by
ordinary least squares (OLS),
\begin{equation}
  e_{i,t}=\alpha_i^{(s)}+\beta_i^{(s)}\,e_{\mathrm{SPY},t}+\varepsilon_{i,t},
\end{equation}
reporting annualised alphas, betas, $t$-statistics and $R^2$.

\subsection{Portfolio construction}
Each strategy is rebalanced monthly (every 21 trading days) using two long-only
optimisers, solved by sequential least-squares quadratic programming (SLSQP):
\begin{align}
  \text{MVP:}\quad & \min_{w}\; w^{\top}\Sigma w
    && \text{s.t.}\;\; \mathbf{1}^{\top}w=1,\;\; w\ge 0, \\[2pt]
  \text{TPF:}\quad & \max_{w}\; \frac{\mu^{\top}w-\rf}{\sqrt{w^{\top}\Sigma w}}
    && \text{s.t.}\;\; \mathbf{1}^{\top}w=1,\;\; w\ge 0 .
\end{align}
The MVP depends only on $\Sigma$; the TPF additionally depends on the expected-return
vector $\mu$. Covariance matrices are estimated with Ledoit--Wolf linear shrinkage and
floored to positive-definiteness; both optimisers fall back to $1/N$ if the numerical
solve fails. For the regime-conditional strategies, $\mu$ and $\Sigma$ are estimated from
the historical days sharing the \emph{current} regime (30-observation minimum, else the
full history). The ML strategies are distinctive in that their $\mu$ is the Random-Forest
\emph{forecast} rather than a historical average. During Crisis regimes, 15\% of capital
is moved to the risk-free asset. This yields ten strategies: passive S\&P~500
buy-and-hold; the $1/N$ equal-weight portfolio; unconditional (``Static'') MVP and TPF;
and regime-conditional MVP and TPF for each of the three classifiers.

\subsection{Backtest, costs and metrics}
The backtest holds weights fixed between monthly rebalances and charges one-way
transaction costs proportional to realised turnover,
\begin{equation}
  \tau_t=\tfrac{1}{2}\sum_i\bigl|w_{i,\text{new}}-w_{i,\text{old}}\bigr|,
\end{equation}
at a baseline of 10 basis points; both net and gross daily return series are retained. We
report annualised return and volatility, the Sharpe and Sortino ratios, maximum drawdown,
Jensen's $\alpha$ and market $\beta$, the Calmar ratio, and the break-even transaction
cost---the total one-way cost (from gross returns) at which a strategy's Sharpe ratio
falls to that of the S\&P~500.

To guard against data snooping we compute, for the best strategy, the \textbf{Deflated
Sharpe Ratio} \citep{bailey2014} and \textbf{White's Reality Check} \citep{white2000}, a
stationary-bootstrap test \citep{politis1994} of the null that the best strategy does not
out-return the benchmark once the full search is accounted for. With daily
relative-performance series $d_{k,t}=r_{k,t}-r_{\mathrm{SPY},t}$, the Reality Check
statistic is
\begin{equation}
  V=\max_{k}\;\sqrt{T}\,\bar d_{k},
  \qquad
  V^{*}_{b}=\max_{k}\;\sqrt{T}\,\bigl(\bar d^{*}_{k,b}-\bar d_{k}\bigr),
  \qquad
  p=\frac{1}{B}\sum_{b=1}^{B}\mathbf{1}\{V^{*}_{b}\ge V\},
\end{equation}
using a mean block length of 10 days and $B=2{,}000$ resamples.

\subsection{Regime characterisation}
The VIX rule assigns 68.6\% of days to Calm (mean VIX 14.8), 23.3\% to Transitional
(23.9), and 8.1\% to Crisis (40.7). The regimes are economically coherent
(Table~\ref{tab:regstats}): the equal-weight sector return averages $+27\%$ annualised in
Calm, $-9\%$ in Transitional and $-90\%$ in Crisis, while volatility rises monotonically.
Figure~\ref{fig:timeline} maps the three classifiers onto the VIX path; the crisis
clusters coincide with the 2008--09 global financial crisis and the early-2020 pandemic
shock. Figure~\ref{fig:heatmap} shows that the classifiers agree most in extreme states
and diverge in the Transitional middle ground, while Figure~\ref{fig:retdist} confirms
that the regime labels separate the cross-section of daily returns into progressively
wider and more left-skewed distributions.

\subsection{Regime-conditional betas}
The conditional CAPM (Table~\ref{tab:betas}, Figure~\ref{fig:betas}) reproduces the
canonical crisis-correlation pattern. Cyclical sectors' market betas rise into
crises---Financials from $1.04$ (Calm) to $1.41$ (Crisis) and Energy from $0.98$ to
$1.27$---so that diversification deteriorates precisely when protection is most valuable
\citep{ang2002}. Defensive sectors remain low-beta throughout (Utilities $\approx0.51$,
Consumer Staples $\approx0.55$, Health Care $\approx0.69$), and regression $R^2$ rises
markedly in crises (e.g.\ Technology $0.73\to0.91$), reflecting the collapse of
idiosyncratic dispersion under stress. This is the economic mechanism a successful
defensive rotation must exploit.

Four patterns emerge. \emph{First}, the value of regime conditioning is overwhelmingly
defensive: every minimum-variance strategy compresses volatility to roughly 14\% (versus
20\% for the S\&P~500) and the maximum drawdown to about $-37\%$ to $-41\%$ (versus
$-60\%$), while nudging the Sharpe ratio above the benchmark's $0.44$
(Figures~\ref{fig:wealth}--\ref{fig:dd}). \emph{Second}, na\"ive diversification
disappoints: the $1/N$ portfolio underperforms the cap-weighted S\&P~500 on every
risk-adjusted metric, a caution against reading \citet{demiguel2009} as an unconditional
endorsement of equal weighting. \emph{Third}, the tangency portfolios bifurcate: those
built on noisy expected-return inputs (HMM-TPF, Static TPF) are erratic---HMM-TPF actually
destroys value---whereas the only tangency portfolio with a disciplined forecast, ML-TPF,
posts the best raw performance in the study. \emph{Fourth}, the minimum-variance
strategies, which never touch the fragile expected-return estimate, are uniformly stable
across all three classifiers, with ML-MVP marginally best. The contrast between the robust
MVP column and the volatile TPF column is a direct illustration of the estimation-risk
critique of mean--variance optimisation. Figure~\ref{fig:weights} traces how the
regime-conditional weights rotate toward defensive sectors and cash as the system enters
Crisis.

\subsection{Transaction costs and break-even}
Measuring turnover directly is essential, because the strategies differ by an order of
magnitude (Table~\ref{tab:perf}). The minimum-variance and VIX strategies trade modestly
(59--108\% per year), but the tangency portfolios churn aggressively---ML-TPF turns over
621\% of capital annually. At 10~bps, the cost drag on ML-TPF is about $0.7\%$ per year,
shaving its Sharpe from $0.65$ (gross) to $0.61$. The break-even analysis
(Figure~\ref{fig:breakeven}) is more discriminating: ML-MVP can absorb $111$~bps of
one-way cost before its Sharpe falls to the S\&P~500's, whereas VIX-TPF tolerates only
$20$~bps and Static/HMM-TPF never beat the benchmark even at zero cost. The cost-robust
strategies are precisely the low-turnover minimum-variance ones.

\subsection{Statistical significance}
No strategy's CAPM alpha is significant at the 5\% level on a single-equation basis;
ML-TPF comes closest at $p=0.059$. The relevant question, however, is whether the
\emph{best of ten searched strategies} is significant, which requires a multiple-testing
correction (Table~\ref{tab:sig}).

\begin{table}[H]
\centering
\caption{Data-snooping corrections for the best strategy (ML-TPF).}
\label{tab:sig}
\small
\begin{tabular}{lll}
\toprule

\midrule
Deflated Sharpe Ratio & $0.977$ (Sharpe $0.61$ vs.\ null $0.15$) & Significant at 95\% \\

\end{tabular}
\floatnote{Notes. The Deflated Sharpe Ratio \citep{bailey2014} reports the probability
that the true Sharpe exceeds the snooping-adjusted null benchmark, correcting for the
number of trials and the skewness and kurtosis of returns. White's Reality Check
\citep{white2000} reports the stationary-bootstrap $p$-value (mean block 10 days,
$B=2{,}000$) of the null that the best strategy does not out-return the S\&P~500.
Source: own calculations.}
\end{table}

The two corrections disagree, and the disagreement is itself the result. The Deflated
Sharpe Ratio of ML-TPF is $0.977$: its annualised Sharpe of $0.61$ comfortably exceeds the
snooping-adjusted null threshold of $0.15$, so its \emph{risk-adjusted} performance is
unlikely to be a selection artefact. Yet White's Reality Check returns a bootstrap
$p$-value of $0.56$, so we \emph{cannot} reject the hypothesis that ML-TPF fails to
out-\emph{return} the S\&P~500 once data snooping and serial dependence are accounted for.
The tests measure different things---the DSR evaluates the Sharpe ratio, the Reality Check
evaluates raw return relative to the benchmark---and their split verdict says that
ML-TPF's edge lives in the \emph{denominator}: it earns a comparable return to the index
with materially less risk, not a higher return. Because the strategy grid is structured
(three classifiers $\times$ two optimisers) rather than a set of independent random
trials, we regard the bootstrap Reality Check as the more conservative and reliable test.

% =====================================================================
\section{Discussion}\label{sec:disc}
% =====================================================================
The results cohere around a single economic message. The conditional-beta evidence shows
that the exploitable structure in the volatility cycle is the rising market sensitivity of
cyclical sectors under stress; the portfolio evidence shows that the strategies which
monetise this structure are the low-turnover, risk-targeting ones; and the significance
evidence shows that what survives scrutiny is risk reduction, not return generation. This
is consistent with a market in which volatility and correlation are far more forecastable
than the sign of returns---a stylised fact underpinning the practical success of
minimum-variance investing \citep{clarke2006} and the difficulty of beating simple
benchmarks with return forecasts \citep{demiguel2009}.

The comparison across classifiers is instructive. The HMM's transition matrix encodes
genuinely useful structure---the obligatory passage through the Transitional state---but
pairing it with a return-timing optimiser (HMM-TPF) amplifies estimation noise into the
worst strategy in the study, while pairing it with minimum-variance (HMM-MVP) yields
solid risk control. The machine-learning pipeline's advantage over the HMM is concentrated
entirely in the ML-TPF cell, and that advantage is exactly the part that fails the Reality
Check. The robust, defensible gains (the MVP column) are available from the crude VIX rule
almost as readily as from the HMM or the forests. For a practitioner, the policy
implication is to prefer a simple, low-turnover, regime-aware minimum-variance overlay
over an elaborate return-timing apparatus.

% =====================================================================
\section{Limitations and Future Work}\label{sec:limit}
% =====================================================================
Several caveats temper the conclusions. (i) The backtest holds weights fixed between
monthly rebalances and measures turnover on a target-to-target basis, abstracting from
intra-month drift; realistic implementation turnover would be somewhat higher,
reinforcing the case against the high-turnover variants. (ii) Transaction costs are a
constant 10~bps proportional charge and exclude market impact. (iii) The crisis cash
overlay and the $20/30$ VIX thresholds are fixed ex ante. (iv) The Deflated Sharpe Ratio
treats the ten strategies as the trial universe, but the true degrees of freedom of the
research process are larger. (v) The HMM is estimated with full covariance matrices and
roughly 200 parameters on initial windows of 504 observations, a regime in which
covariance estimates are noisy. Promising directions include soft-information portfolios
that weight by regime \emph{posterior probabilities}, a Hidden semi-Markov specification
with explicit duration distributions, and an out-of-sample combination of the three
classifiers.

% =====================================================================
\section{Conclusion}\label{sec:concl}
% =====================================================================
Conditioning a US sector-rotation strategy on inferred market regimes---whether by a
simple VIX rule, a Gaussian Hidden Markov Model, or a machine-learning
clustering-plus-forecasting pipeline---delivers a large, robust, and economically
meaningful reduction in portfolio risk: drawdowns fall from roughly $-60\%$ to $-37\%$ and
Sharpe ratios rise modestly above a passive benchmark, at low and cost-tolerant turnover.
The same cannot be said for return enhancement. The single strategy that appears to add
alpha trades six times its capital per year and, once data snooping is corrected for,
cannot be shown to out-return the S\&P~500. The sophistication of the regime model matters
far less than the choice of portfolio objective: across all three classifiers, the
defensible value comes from minimum-variance risk targeting, not from timing returns. In
this dataset, the intelligence in ``intelligent'' regime allocation is best spent on
managing risk.
