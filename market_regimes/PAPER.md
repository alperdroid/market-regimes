# A Comparative Taxonomy of Market Regimes: Machine Learning versus Hidden Markov Models in US Tactical Sector Allocation

## Abstract

We compare three families of market-regime classifiers — a rule-based volatility
trigger, a Gaussian Hidden Markov Model (HMM), and a decoupled machine-learning
pipeline (Gaussian Mixture clustering followed by regime-specialist Random Forest
return forecasters) — and ask whether conditioning a US sector-rotation strategy on
the inferred regime improves out-of-sample, after-cost performance relative to a
passive S&P 500 benchmark. Using daily data on nine SPDR sector ETFs from 2004 to
2025, all regime models are estimated in a strict expanding-window walk-forward
design, and portfolios are formed each month by regime-conditional minimum-variance
(MVP) and maximum-Sharpe tangency (TPF) optimisation with Ledoit–Wolf covariance
shrinkage. We find that regime conditioning delivers a large and robust reduction in
volatility and drawdown — the minimum-variance strategies cut the maximum drawdown
from roughly −60% (S&P 500) to −37% while modestly raising the Sharpe ratio — but
that the apparent *return* advantage of the best machine-learning strategy does not
survive a data-snooping correction (White's Reality Check, p = 0.56), even though its
risk-adjusted Sharpe ratio does (Deflated Sharpe Ratio = 0.98). We conclude that the
economic value of regime-based tactical allocation in this setting is concentrated in
risk control rather than in abnormal returns, and that high-turnover return-timing
variants are largely competed away by transaction costs.

---

## 1. Introduction

A long tradition in empirical finance holds that asset returns are generated by a
small number of recurring "states of the world" — calm bull markets, turbulent
transitions, and crises — with sharply different first and second moments
(Hamilton, 1989; Ang and Bekaert, 2002; Guidolin and Timmermann, 2007). If such
regimes are identifiable in real time, an investor could in principle rotate across
assets to harvest the favourable states and de-risk in the unfavourable ones. This
paper studies that proposition for US equity *sectors*, the most liquid vehicle for
expressing tactical macro views, and confronts a practical question that much of the
academic literature elides: once a regime model is estimated only on information
available at the time of trading, and once realistic transaction costs are charged,
does regime conditioning actually beat a passive benchmark?

We make three contributions. First, we place three structurally distinct regime
classifiers on a common, strictly out-of-sample footing: a transparent VIX threshold
rule, a Gaussian HMM that explicitly models regime *persistence*, and a two-stage
machine-learning pipeline that separates regime *identification* (unsupervised
clustering) from regime-conditional *return forecasting* (supervised ensembles). The
comparison is designed so that the VIX-vs-HMM contrast isolates the value of a
data-driven latent-state model over a fixed rule, while the HMM-vs-ML contrast
isolates the value of modelling temporal dependence (the HMM's Markov transition
matrix) versus a memoryless mixture. Second, we evaluate each regime signal through
two portfolio lenses — minimum-variance (which depends only on the covariance matrix)
and maximum-Sharpe tangency (which additionally depends on expected returns) — so as
to separate the value of risk estimation from the value of return forecasting. Third,
and most importantly for the credibility of the exercise, we measure portfolio
turnover directly, charge transaction costs, and subject the leading strategy to
formal multiple-testing corrections, because evaluating ten strategies on one sample
mechanically inflates the best in-sample result (Harvey, Liu and Zhu, 2016).

Our headline finding is a clean dissociation. Regime conditioning reliably *reduces
risk*: across all three classifiers, the minimum-variance variants achieve Sharpe
ratios of 0.46–0.50 against the S&P 500's 0.44 at roughly two-thirds of the
volatility and three-fifths of the drawdown, with low turnover that is robust to
plausible trading costs. But the one strategy that appears to *add return* — the
machine-learning tangency portfolio, with a 9.5% annualised return and a 4.3% CAPM
alpha — trades more than six times its capital per year, and its return advantage
over the S&P 500 is statistically indistinguishable from chance once data snooping is
accounted for. The value of "intelligence" in regime modelling, in this dataset, is
defensive.

## 2. Related Literature

**Regime-switching models.** The Markov-switching framework of Hamilton (1989)
formalised the idea that macroeconomic and financial time series alternate between
discrete states with state-dependent dynamics. Ang and Bekaert (2002) and Guidolin
and Timmermann (2007) applied multivariate regime-switching models to international
equity allocation and documented that correlations and volatilities rise sharply in
"bear" regimes, eroding diversification precisely when it is most needed. The Gaussian
HMM we employ is the canonical estimation device for such latent-state models, fitted
by the Baum–Welch expectation–maximisation algorithm (Baum et al., 1970; Dempster,
Laird and Rubin, 1977) and decoded by the Viterbi (1967) algorithm.

**Volatility as a regime proxy.** The CBOE VIX index, the risk-neutral expected
volatility of the S&P 500 over the next 30 days (Whaley, 2009), is the most widely
used real-time stress gauge. Practitioner and academic conventions treat VIX below 20
as calm, 20–30 as elevated, and above 30 as crisis (Whaley, 2009; Bloom, 2009), a
mapping we adopt as our transparent benchmark classifier. Bloom (2009) shows that
volatility shocks are themselves a driver of real and financial dynamics, motivating
the use of the *change* in VIX as a regime feature.

**Machine learning for regimes and returns.** Gaussian Mixture Models provide a
soft-clustering analogue to the HMM without the Markov persistence assumption
(McLachlan and Peel, 2000), and have been used to label volatility regimes. Random
Forests (Breiman, 2001) are a standard non-linear ensemble for return and risk
prediction (Gu, Kelly and Xiu, 2020), valued for their robustness to noise and their
interpretable feature importances. Our pipeline deliberately *decouples* clustering
from forecasting, training one specialist forest per regime — a design in the spirit
of regime-dependent predictive regressions but using a flexible non-parametric learner.

**Portfolio construction and estimation risk.** Markowitz (1952) mean–variance
optimisation is notoriously sensitive to estimation error in expected returns, to the
point that the naïve 1/N portfolio is hard to beat out of sample (DeMiguel, Garlappi
and Uppal, 2009). Two responses inform our design: minimum-variance investing, which
avoids the expected-return input entirely and has delivered strong risk-adjusted
performance empirically (Clarke, de Silva and Thorley, 2006), and covariance
shrinkage, for which Ledoit and Wolf (2004) derive an optimal linear combination of
the sample covariance and a structured target. We use both.

**Performance evaluation under data snooping.** Because we search over many
strategies, in-sample outperformance is upward biased. White (2000) introduced the
Reality Check, a bootstrap test of whether the *best* of many strategies beats a
benchmark, implemented here with the stationary bootstrap of Politis and Romano
(1994). Bailey and López de Prado (2014) propose the Deflated Sharpe Ratio, which
benchmarks a strategy's Sharpe ratio against the expected maximum Sharpe attainable by
chance across the trials searched. Harvey, Liu and Zhu (2016) make the general case
for raising significance thresholds in light of the multiplicity of tested
strategies. We apply all three ideas.

## 3. Data

The sample comprises daily data from January 2004 to December 2025 (5,533 trading
days), sourced from Yahoo! Finance and the Federal Reserve Economic Database (FRED)
and cached locally for reproducibility (`data/loader.py`).

**Asset universe.** The nine SPDR Select Sector ETFs — Technology (XLK), Consumer
Discretionary (XLY), Financials (XLF), Industrials (XLI), Materials (XLB), Energy
(XLE), Consumer Staples (XLP), Health Care (XLV), and Utilities (XLU) — span the
investable US equity market, with SPDR S&P 500 (SPY) as the benchmark. We use
dividend- and split-adjusted closing prices.

**State and macro variables.** The CBOE VIX index proxies market stress. The
risk-free rate is the 13-week Treasury-bill annualised yield (`^IRX`), converted to a
daily decimal rate. Two macro–financial conditioning variables are drawn from FRED:
the TED spread (`TEDRATE`), a funding-stress indicator, and the 10-year–2-year
Treasury term spread (`T10Y2Y`), a leading indicator of the business cycle. The loader
degrades gracefully if a FRED series is unavailable, substituting documented proxies.

All series are forward-filled and aligned to the union of SPY trading days. Log
returns are computed as r_{i,t} = ln(P_{i,t}/P_{i,t−1}), and excess returns as
e_{i,t} = r_{i,t} − r^f_t (`data/features.py`).

## 4. Methodology

### 4.1 Feature construction

Two feature sets are built (`data/features.py`). The *regime* features used by the HMM
and the Gaussian Mixture are the daily change in VIX, ΔVIX_t = VIX_t − VIX_{t−1},
stacked with the nine sector log returns (a 10-dimensional vector). The *forecasting*
features used by the Random Forests are richer: the VIX level, ΔVIX, the TED spread,
the term spread, and sector log returns lagged 1, 2, 3, and 5 days. This separation
encodes an economic prior — *which* regime prevails is driven primarily by
volatility, whereas *what returns to expect within* a regime depends on a broader
macro and momentum information set.

### 4.2 Regime identification

**(a) VIX rule.** The transparent benchmark assigns each day to Calm (VIX < 20),
Transitional (20 ≤ VIX < 30), or Crisis (VIX ≥ 30), following Whaley (2009) and Bloom
(2009) (`regimes/vix_classifier.py`). These fixed thresholds are preferred to
percentile cuts because they carry a stable economic interpretation across the sample.

**(b) Hidden Markov Model.** We fit a three-state Gaussian HMM in which the observed
feature vector is, conditional on the latent state s_t ∈ {0,1,2}, multivariate normal,

  x_t | s_t = k ∼ N(μ_k, Σ_k),

with full state-specific covariance matrices Σ_k and a first-order Markov transition
matrix A, A_{ij} = P(s_{t+1}=j | s_t=i) (`regimes/hmm_model.py`). Parameters are
estimated by Baum–Welch EM; because EM converges only to local optima, we take the
best of twenty random restarts by log-likelihood. The most probable state path is
recovered by Viterbi decoding. Crucially, HMM state indices are not identified by the
likelihood — the "label-switching" problem — so we sort the states by their
state-conditional mean ΔVIX, fixing state 0 as the lowest-volatility (Calm) and state
2 as the highest (Crisis) and permuting the transition matrix, means and covariances
accordingly. The transition matrix then has a direct economic reading as regime
persistence and expected duration.

**(c) Machine-learning pipeline.** The ML arm decouples identification from
forecasting (`regimes/ml_pipeline.py`). Step 1 fits a three-component Gaussian Mixture
on the regime features and assigns each day a hard cluster label, again sorted by
ΔVIX for identifiability; unlike the HMM, the mixture is memoryless, so the HMM-vs-ML
comparison isolates the value of modelling persistence. Step 2 trains three
independent multi-output Random Forest regressors — one per regime — each fitted only
on the days the mixture assigned to that regime and each predicting the next day's
nine sector returns from the forecasting features (300 trees, depth 5, minimum leaf
size 20). At prediction time the mixture selects the regime and the corresponding
forest produces the return forecast that feeds the optimiser. The shallow, heavily
regularised forests guard against overfitting the noisy return target.

**(d) Walk-forward estimation.** All learned models (HMM, GMM, forests) are fitted in
an *expanding-window walk-forward* (`fit_hmm_walkforward`, `fit_ml_walkforward`). A
model is trained on all data up to time t and used to label/forecast the subsequent
year; it is then refitted with the year of new data appended. We require a 504-day
(two-year) minimum training window and refit every 252 days. This guarantees that all
regime labels and return forecasts used in the backtest are strictly out-of-sample,
the single most important safeguard against look-ahead bias. The construction of the
forecasting target — next-day returns aligned to current-day features — is likewise
strictly causal.

### 4.3 Regime-conditional CAPM

To characterise the economic content of the regimes, we estimate, for each sector i
and each (VIX-defined) regime s, the conditional market model by OLS
(`capm/beta_analysis.py`),

  e_{i,t} = α_i^{(s)} + β_i^{(s)} · e_{SPY,t} + ε_{i,t},

reporting annualised alphas, betas, t-statistics and R². This quantifies how each
sector's market sensitivity changes across the volatility cycle.

### 4.4 Portfolio construction

Each strategy is rebalanced monthly (every 21 trading days). Two long-only optimisers
are used (`portfolio/optimizer.py`), both solved by sequential quadratic programming
(SLSQP) with a full-investment constraint and non-negativity:

- **Minimum-variance (MVP):** min_w w′Σw s.t. 1′w = 1, w ≥ 0. Depends only on Σ.
- **Tangency / maximum-Sharpe (TPF):** max_w (μ′w − r^f)/√(w′Σw) s.t. 1′w = 1, w ≥ 0.
  Depends additionally on the expected-return vector μ.

Covariance matrices are estimated with Ledoit–Wolf linear shrinkage and floored to
positive-definiteness (`portfolio/ledoit_wolf.py`); both optimisers fall back to the
1/N portfolio if the numerical solve fails, and the tangency portfolio falls back to
minimum-variance when no asset has a positive expected excess return. For the
regime-conditional strategies, μ and Σ are estimated from the *historical days sharing
the current regime* (with a 30-observation minimum, else the full history), so that
the moments reflect the prevailing state. The ML strategies are distinctive in that
their μ is the Random-Forest *forecast* rather than a historical average. During Crisis
regimes, 15% of capital is moved to the risk-free asset (`apply_crisis_cash`), a
simple de-risking overlay.

This yields ten strategies: a passive S&P 500 buy-and-hold; the 1/N equal-weight
portfolio; unconditional ("Static") MVP and TPF that ignore regimes; and
regime-conditional MVP and TPF for each of the three classifiers (VIX, HMM, ML).

### 4.5 Backtest, costs and metrics

The walk-forward backtest (`portfolio/backtest.py`) holds weights fixed between
monthly rebalances and charges one-way transaction costs proportional to realised
turnover, τ_t = ½·Σ_i|w_{i,new} − w_{i,old}|, at a baseline of 10 basis points; both
the net and gross daily return series are retained. We report standard metrics
(`performance/metrics.py`): annualised return and volatility, the Sharpe and Sortino
ratios, maximum drawdown, Jensen's alpha and market beta from a CAPM regression on
SPY, the Calmar ratio, and the break-even transaction cost — the total one-way cost,
computed from gross returns, at which a strategy's Sharpe ratio falls to that of the
S&P 500.

To guard against data snooping we additionally compute, for the best strategy, the
**Deflated Sharpe Ratio** (Bailey and López de Prado, 2014), which evaluates the
strategy's Sharpe against the expected maximum Sharpe achievable by chance given the
number of trials and the cross-sectional dispersion of their Sharpe ratios while
correcting for the skewness and kurtosis of returns, and **White's (2000) Reality
Check**, a stationary-bootstrap test (Politis and Romano, 1994; mean block 10 days,
2,000 resamples) of the null that the best strategy does not out-return the S&P 500
once the full search is accounted for.

## 5. Results

### 5.1 Regime characterisation

The VIX rule assigns 68.6% of days to Calm (mean VIX 14.8), 23.3% to Transitional
(23.9), and 8.1% to Crisis (40.7). The regimes are economically coherent: the
equal-weight sector return averages +27% annualised in Calm, −9% in Transitional and
−90% in Crisis, while volatility rises monotonically from 10% to 46%.

The HMM recovers a more balanced and highly persistent partition (Calm 2,302 days,
Transitional 2,220, Crisis 507). Its sorted transition matrix is

|        | Calm  | Trans | Crisis |
|--------|-------|-------|--------|
| Calm   | 0.895 | 0.105 | 0.000  |
| Trans  | 0.162 | 0.807 | 0.030  |
| Crisis | 0.000 | 0.166 | 0.834  |

Three features stand out. States are persistent (diagonal probabilities 0.81–0.90),
implying expected durations of roughly 5–10 days for a single visit but much longer
sojourns in practice through self-transition. The economy never jumps *directly*
between Calm and Crisis: it must pass through the Transitional state in both
directions, exactly the "ramp-up / ramp-down" dynamic that a memoryless classifier
cannot capture. The Gaussian-mixture labels used by the ML pipeline are, by
construction, similar in cross-section (Calm 3,022, Transitional 1,612, Crisis 389)
but lack this temporal structure.

### 5.2 Regime-conditional betas

The conditional CAPM (`results/beta_summary.csv`) reproduces the canonical
crisis-correlation pattern. Cyclical sectors' market betas rise into crises —
Financials from 1.04 (Calm) to 1.41 (Crisis) and Energy from 0.98 to 1.27 — so that
diversification deteriorates precisely when protection is most valuable, as in Ang and
Bekaert (2002). Defensive sectors remain low-beta throughout (Utilities ≈ 0.51,
Consumer Staples ≈ 0.55, Health Care ≈ 0.69), and regression R² rises markedly in
crises (e.g. Technology 0.73 → 0.91), reflecting the well-documented collapse of
idiosyncratic dispersion and rise of common factor risk under stress. This is the
economic mechanism a successful defensive rotation must exploit: tilt toward low-beta
sectors and cash as the system transitions toward Crisis.

### 5.3 Portfolio performance

2006–December 2025, 5,023 days), net of 10 bps one-way costs.

Four patterns emerge. *First*, the value of regime conditioning is overwhelmingly
defensive. Every minimum-variance strategy compresses volatility to roughly 14%
(versus 20% for the S&P 500) and the maximum drawdown to about −37% to −41% (versus
−60%), while nudging the Sharpe ratio above the benchmark's 0.44. *Second*, naïve
diversification disappoints: the 1/N portfolio underperforms the cap-weighted S&P 500
on every risk-adjusted metric, consistent with the large-cap, momentum-laden character
of the post-2008 US market — a caution against reading DeMiguel, Garlappi and Uppal
(2009) as an unconditional endorsement of equal weighting. *Third*, the tangency
portfolios bifurcate: those built on noisy expected-return inputs (HMM-TPF, Static
TPF) are erratic — HMM-TPF actually destroys value, with a Sharpe of 0.25 and a
negative alpha — whereas the only tangency portfolio with a disciplined forecast,
ML-TPF, posts the best raw performance in the study (9.5% return, 0.61 Sharpe, 4.3%
alpha). *Fourth*, the minimum-variance strategies, which never touch the fragile
expected-return estimate, are uniformly stable across all three classifiers, with
ML-MVP marginally best (Sharpe 0.50, alpha 1.9%). The contrast between the robust MVP
column and the volatile TPF column is a direct empirical illustration of the
estimation-risk critique of mean–variance optimisation.

### 5.4 Transaction costs and break-even

Measuring turnover directly (rather than assuming it) is essential, because the
strategies differ by an order of magnitude. The minimum-variance and VIX strategies
trade modestly (59–108% per year), but the tangency portfolios churn aggressively —
ML-TPF turns over 621% of capital annually, Static TPF 327%, HMM-TPF 496%. At 10 bps,
the cost drag on ML-TPF is about 0.7% per year, shaving its Sharpe from 0.65 (gross)
to 0.61. The break-even analysis (Table 1, final column; Figure 10) is more
discriminating: ML-MVP can absorb 111 bps of one-way cost before its Sharpe falls to
the S&P 500's, whereas VIX-TPF tolerates only 20 bps despite a respectable headline
Sharpe, and Static TPF and HMM-TPF never beat the benchmark even at zero cost. *The
cost-robust strategies are precisely the low-turnover minimum-variance ones.* The
flashier return-timing variants are largely an artefact of frictionless accounting.

### 5.5 Statistical significance

Table 1 already shows that no strategy's CAPM alpha is significant at the 5% level on
a single-equation basis; ML-TPF comes closest at p = 0.059. But the relevant question
is whether the *best of ten searched strategies* is significant, which requires a
multiple-testing correction (`results/significance_tests.csv`).

The two corrections disagree, and the disagreement is itself the result. The Deflated
Sharpe Ratio of ML-TPF is 0.977: its annualised Sharpe of 0.61 comfortably exceeds the
snooping-adjusted null threshold of 0.15, so its *risk-adjusted* performance is
unlikely to be a selection artefact. Yet White's Reality Check returns a bootstrap
p-value of 0.56, so we *cannot* reject the hypothesis that ML-TPF fails to out-*return*
the S&P 500 once data snooping and serial dependence are accounted for. The two tests
measure different things — the DSR evaluates the Sharpe ratio, the Reality Check
evaluates raw return relative to the benchmark — and their split verdict says that
ML-TPF's edge lives in the *denominator*: it earns a comparable return to the index
with materially less risk, not a higher return. Because the strategy grid is
structured (three classifiers × two optimisers) rather than a set of independent
random trials, we regard the bootstrap Reality Check as the more conservative and
reliable test, and read the evidence as: regime-based allocation improves
risk-adjusted outcomes but does not demonstrably generate alpha.

## 6. Discussion

The results cohere around a single economic message. The conditional-beta evidence
(Section 5.2) shows that the exploitable structure in the volatility cycle is the
rising market sensitivity of cyclical sectors under stress; the portfolio evidence
(Sections 5.3–5.4) shows that the strategies which monetise this structure are the
low-turnover, risk-targeting ones, not the high-turnover return-timing ones; and the
significance evidence (Section 5.5) shows that what survives scrutiny is risk
reduction, not return generation. This is consistent with a market in which
volatility and correlation are far more forecastable than the sign of returns — a
stylised fact that underpins the practical success of minimum-variance and risk-parity
investing (Clarke, de Silva and Thorley, 2006) and the difficulty of beating simple
benchmarks with return forecasts (DeMiguel, Garlappi and Uppal, 2009).

The comparison across classifiers is instructive. The HMM's transition matrix
encodes genuinely useful structure — the obligatory passage through the Transitional
state — but pairing it with a return-timing optimiser (HMM-TPF) amplifies estimation
noise into the worst strategy in the study, while pairing it with minimum-variance
(HMM-MVP) yields solid, unspectacular risk control. The machine-learning pipeline's
advantage over the HMM is concentrated entirely in the ML-TPF cell, and that advantage
is exactly the part that fails the Reality Check. In other words, the additional
modelling sophistication buys a better *return forecast* whose statistical reality is
unproven, while the robust, defensible gains (the MVP column) are available from the
crude VIX rule almost as readily as from the HMM or the forests. For a practitioner,
the policy implication is to prefer a simple, low-turnover, regime-aware
minimum-variance overlay over an elaborate return-timing apparatus.

---

## 7. Limitations and Future Work

Several caveats temper the conclusions. (i) The backtest holds weights fixed between
monthly rebalances and therefore measures turnover on a target-to-target basis,
abstracting from intra-month drift; realistic implementation turnover — and hence
cost drag — would be somewhat higher, reinforcing the case against the high-turnover
variants. (ii) Transaction costs are modelled as a constant 10 bps proportional charge
and exclude market impact, which would penalise the 600%-turnover strategies further.
(iii) The crisis cash overlay and the 20/30 VIX thresholds are fixed ex ante; while
literature-grounded, they are not optimised, and a fuller study would test their
sensitivity. (iv) The Deflated Sharpe Ratio treats the ten strategies as the trial
universe, but the true degrees of freedom of the research process (feature choices,
window lengths, hyperparameters) are larger, so even the DSR's favourable verdict
should be read cautiously. (v) The HMM is estimated with full covariance matrices and
roughly 200 parameters on initial windows of 504 observations, a regime in which
covariance estimates are noisy; shrinkage on the HMM emissions, or a more parsimonious
covariance structure, is a natural extension. Promising directions include
soft-information portfolios that weight by regime *posterior probabilities* rather than
hard labels, a Hidden semi-Markov specification with explicit duration distributions,
and an out-of-sample combination of the three classifiers.

---

## 8. Conclusion

Conditioning a US sector-rotation strategy on inferred market regimes — whether by a
simple VIX rule, a Gaussian Hidden Markov Model, or a machine-learning clustering-plus-
forecasting pipeline — delivers a large, robust, and economically meaningful reduction
in portfolio risk: drawdowns fall from roughly −60% to −37% and Sharpe ratios rise
modestly above a passive benchmark, at low and cost-tolerant turnover. The same cannot
be said for return enhancement. The single strategy that appears to add alpha, the
machine-learning tangency portfolio, trades six times its capital per year and, once
data snooping is corrected for, cannot be shown to out-return the S&P 500. The
sophistication of the regime model matters far less than the choice of portfolio
objective: across all three classifiers, the defensible value comes from minimum-
variance risk targeting, not from timing returns. In this dataset, the intelligence in
"intelligent" regime allocation is best spent on managing risk.
