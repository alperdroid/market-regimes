# A Comparative Taxonomy of Market Regimes: Machine Learning versus Hidden Markov Models in US Tactical Sector Allocation

## Abstract

We compare three families of market-regime classifiers — a rule-based volatility trigger,
a Gaussian Hidden Markov Model (HMM), and an unsupervised Gaussian Mixture Model (GMM) —
all feeding an *identical* regime-conditional portfolio construction, and ask whether
conditioning a US sector-rotation strategy on the inferred regime improves out-of-sample,
after-cost performance relative to a passive S&P 500 benchmark. Using daily data on nine
SPDR sector ETFs from 2004 to 2025, all regime models are estimated in a strict
expanding-window walk-forward design, and portfolios are formed each month by
regime-conditional minimum-variance (MVP) and maximum-Sharpe tangency (TPF) optimisation
with Ledoit–Wolf covariance shrinkage. We find that regime conditioning delivers a large
and robust reduction in volatility and drawdown — the minimum-variance strategies cut the
maximum drawdown from roughly −60% (S&P 500) to about −37% and raise the Sharpe ratio from
0.44 to 0.48–0.54 — but that this gain comes entirely from risk targeting. Because all three
classifiers feed an identical portfolio construction, the tangency (return-timing) variants
fail uniformly, and no strategy out-returns the benchmark once data snooping is corrected
for (White's Reality Check p = 0.98); the best strategy's risk-adjusted edge is only
marginally significant after deflation and comes from lower risk, not higher return. We
conclude that the economic value of regime-based tactical
allocation in this setting is concentrated in risk control rather than abnormal returns, and
that it is largely insensitive to the sophistication of the regime classifier.

## 1. Introduction

A long tradition in empirical finance holds that asset returns are generated by a small
number of recurring "states of the world" — calm bull markets, turbulent transitions, and
crises — with sharply different first and second moments (Hamilton, 1989; Ang and Bekaert,
2002; Guidolin and Timmermann, 2007). If such regimes are identifiable in real time, an
investor could in principle rotate across assets to harvest the favourable states and
de-risk in the unfavourable ones. This paper studies that proposition for US equity
*sectors* and confronts a practical question that much of the academic literature elides:
once a regime model is estimated only on information available at the time of trading, and
once realistic transaction costs are charged, does regime conditioning actually beat a
passive benchmark?

**Objective and research question.** The objective is to quantify, on a strictly
out-of-sample and after-cost basis, the economic value added by market-regime conditioning,
and to decompose it into a *risk* component and a *return* component. We address three
research questions. (RQ1) Does a data-driven latent-state model (HMM or GMM) identify
regimes more usefully than a transparent VIX rule? (RQ2) Is any incremental value
attributable to regime *persistence*, isolated by contrasting the Markovian HMM with a
memoryless mixture? (RQ3) Does the best-performing strategy survive a formal data-snooping
correction, or is its outperformance a selection artefact?

**Design.** The central methodological choice is that all three classifiers feed an
*identical* downstream portfolio construction: the mean vector and covariance matrix are
estimated from the historical days assigned to the prevailing regime, and the same
optimisers and cost model are applied. No auxiliary return-forecasting model is interposed.
Consequently, any performance difference across strategies is attributable to the
regime-*labelling* method itself, which is precisely what makes the comparison clean.

**Headline finding.** Regime conditioning reliably *reduces risk*: the minimum-variance
variants achieve Sharpe ratios of 0.48–0.54 against the S&P 500's 0.44, at roughly two-thirds
the volatility and three-fifths the drawdown, with low and cost-tolerant turnover. But once
every method shares one portfolio construction, the return-timing (tangency) variants fail
uniformly — several destroy value — and no strategy out-returns the benchmark after a
data-snooping correction. The value of "intelligence" in regime modelling, in this dataset,
is defensive, and it is captured almost as well by the crude VIX rule as by the HMM or the
GMM.

## 2. Related Literature

**Regime-switching models.** The Markov-switching framework of Hamilton (1989) formalised
the idea that financial time series alternate between discrete states with state-dependent
dynamics. Ang and Bekaert (2002) and Guidolin and Timmermann (2007) applied multivariate
regime-switching models to equity allocation and documented that correlations and
volatilities rise sharply in "bear" regimes, eroding diversification precisely when it is
most needed. The Gaussian HMM is the canonical estimation device, fitted by the Baum–Welch
EM algorithm (Baum et al., 1970; Dempster, Laird and Rubin, 1977) and decoded by the Viterbi
(1967) algorithm.

**Volatility as a regime proxy.** The CBOE VIX index, the risk-neutral expected volatility
of the S&P 500 over the next 30 days (Whaley, 2009), is the most widely used real-time stress
gauge. Conventions treat VIX below 20 as calm, 20–30 as elevated, and above 30 as crisis
(Whaley, 2009; Bloom, 2009), a mapping we adopt as our transparent benchmark classifier.
Bloom (2009) shows volatility shocks are themselves a driver of dynamics, motivating the use
of the *change* in VIX as a feature.

**Unsupervised clustering of regimes.** Gaussian Mixture Models provide a soft-clustering
analogue to the HMM without the Markov persistence assumption (McLachlan and Peel, 2000).
Fitting a finite Gaussian mixture to the joint distribution of volatility changes and sector
returns partitions the sample into latent states in the same emission family as the HMM, but
treating each observation independently. Comparing the two therefore isolates the
contribution of the temporal (Markov) structure while holding the distributional assumptions
fixed.

**Portfolio construction and estimation risk.** Markowitz (1952) mean–variance optimisation
is notoriously sensitive to estimation error in expected returns, to the point that the naïve
1/N portfolio is hard to beat out of sample (DeMiguel, Garlappi and Uppal, 2009). Two
responses inform our design: minimum-variance investing, which avoids the expected-return
input entirely and has delivered strong risk-adjusted performance (Clarke, de Silva and
Thorley, 2006), and covariance shrinkage, for which Ledoit and Wolf (2004) derive an optimal
linear combination of the sample covariance and a structured target.

**Performance evaluation under data snooping.** Because we search over many strategies,
in-sample outperformance is upward biased. White (2000) introduced the Reality Check, a
bootstrap test of whether the *best* of many strategies beats a benchmark, implemented with
the stationary bootstrap of Politis and Romano (1994). Bailey and López de Prado (2014)
propose the Deflated Sharpe Ratio, which benchmarks a strategy's Sharpe against the expected
maximum Sharpe attainable by chance across the trials searched. Harvey, Liu and Zhu (2016)
make the general case for raising significance thresholds in light of the multiplicity of
tested strategies.

## 3. Data

The sample comprises daily data from January 2004 to December 2025 (5,533 trading days),
sourced from Yahoo! Finance and FRED and cached locally for reproducibility.

**Asset universe.** The nine SPDR Select Sector ETFs — Technology (XLK), Consumer
Discretionary (XLY), Financials (XLF), Industrials (XLI), Materials (XLB), Energy (XLE),
Consumer Staples (XLP), Health Care (XLV), Utilities (XLU) — with SPDR S&P 500 (SPY) as the
benchmark; dividend- and split-adjusted closing prices.

**State and macro variables.** The CBOE VIX proxies market stress. The risk-free rate is the
13-week T-bill yield (`^IRX`), converted to a daily decimal rate. Log returns are
`r = ln(P_t/P_{t-1})` and excess returns are `e = r − r_f`.

## 4. Methodology

### 4.1 Feature construction

Both data-driven classifiers operate on the same regime-feature vector: the daily change in
VIX, ΔVIX, stacked with the nine sector log returns (a 10-dimensional vector). Volatility —
through the level and change of the VIX — is the primary driver of which regime prevails, so
a parsimonious common feature set keeps the HMM and the GMM comparable and guards against
overfitting the state assignment.

### 4.2 Regime identification

**(a) VIX rule.** Each day is assigned to Calm (VIX < 20), Transitional (20 ≤ VIX < 30), or
Crisis (VIX ≥ 30), following Whaley (2009) and Bloom (2009).

**(b) Hidden Markov Model.** A three-state Gaussian HMM with full state-specific covariances
and a first-order Markov transition matrix A, x_t | s_t=k ~ N(μ_k, Σ_k). Parameters are
estimated by Baum–Welch EM (best of twenty random restarts by log-likelihood). Out-of-sample
labels use the *filtered* state probability P(s_t | x_1…x_t) from the forward algorithm —
conditioning only on past and present, unlike the Viterbi path or smoothed states, which use
the whole sequence and would leak future information into a real-time label — putting the HMM
on the same strictly causal footing as the memoryless GMM (Hamilton 1989; Ang & Timmermann
2012). States are sorted by their conditional mean ΔVIX (fixing 0 = Calm, 2 = Crisis) to
resolve label-switching.

**(c) Gaussian Mixture Model.** The machine-learning classifier is a three-component Gaussian
Mixture fitted on the regime features, assigning each day a hard cluster label sorted by ΔVIX
for identifiability. Unlike the HMM, the mixture is *memoryless*: it has no transition matrix
and treats each day independently — which is the point, since contrasting GMM with HMM
isolates the marginal value of modelling persistence while holding the Gaussian emission
structure fixed. The GMM produces regime *labels only*; the portfolio moments it feeds are
estimated, exactly as for the VIX rule and the HMM, from the historical days assigned to the
prevailing regime. No return-forecasting model is interposed.

**(d) Walk-forward estimation.** Both learned models are fitted in an expanding-window
walk-forward: trained on all data up to time t, used to label the subsequent year, then
refitted with the new year appended (504-day minimum window, yearly refit). All regime labels
used in the backtest are strictly out-of-sample.

### 4.3 Regime-conditional CAPM

For each sector i and each VIX regime s, the conditional market model
`e_{i,t} = α_i^(s) + β_i^(s)·e_{SPY,t} + ε_{i,t}` is estimated by OLS, reporting annualised
alphas, betas, t-statistics and R².

### 4.4 Portfolio construction

Each strategy is rebalanced monthly (21 days) using two long-only optimisers solved by SLSQP:
minimum-variance (MVP, depends only on Σ) and maximum-Sharpe tangency (TPF, additionally
depends on μ). Covariances use Ledoit–Wolf shrinkage with eigenvalue flooring; both fall back
to 1/N if the solve fails. For the regime-conditional strategies, μ and Σ are estimated from
the historical days sharing the *current* regime (30-observation minimum, else full history),
so that all three classifiers feed an identical portfolio construction and differ only in how
they label the prevailing regime. During Crisis regimes 15% of capital moves to cash. This
yields the strategies: SPY buy-and-hold; 1/N; unconditional ("Static") MVP and TPF; and
regime-conditional MVP and TPF for each classifier. (The code additionally evaluates a
majority-vote Ensemble of the three classifiers; the paper focuses on VIX, HMM and GMM.)

### 4.5 Backtest, costs and metrics

Weights are held fixed between monthly rebalances; one-way transaction costs proportional to
realised turnover are charged at 10 bps, and both net and gross series are retained. We report
annualised return and volatility, Sharpe and Sortino, maximum drawdown, Jensen's α and market
β, the Calmar ratio, and the break-even transaction cost (the total one-way cost, from gross
returns, at which a strategy's Sharpe falls to the S&P 500's). To guard against data snooping
we compute the **Deflated Sharpe Ratio** (Bailey and López de Prado, 2014) and **White's
Reality Check** (White, 2000) via the stationary bootstrap (Politis and Romano, 1994; mean
block 10 days, 2,000 resamples).

## 5. Results

### 5.1 Regime characterisation

The VIX rule assigns 68.6% of days to Calm (mean VIX 14.8), 23.3% to Transitional (23.9),
8.1% to Crisis (40.7); the equal-weight sector return averages +27% annualised in Calm, −9% in
Transitional and −90% in Crisis. The HMM recovers a persistent partition (Calm 2,668 days,
Transitional 2,220, Crisis 507) whose sorted transition matrix never jumps directly between
Calm and Crisis — the "ramp-up / ramp-down" dynamic a memoryless classifier cannot capture.
The GMM labels (Calm 3,078, Transitional 1,513, Crisis 438) lack this temporal structure.

### 5.2 Regime-conditional betas

The conditional CAPM reproduces the canonical crisis-correlation pattern: cyclical betas rise
into crises (Financials 1.04 → 1.41, Energy 0.98 → 1.27), defensives stay low (Utilities
≈ 0.51, Staples ≈ 0.55, Health Care ≈ 0.69), and R² rises markedly under stress (e.g.
Technology 0.73 → 0.91). This is the mechanism a defensive rotation must exploit.

Four patterns emerge. *First*, the value of regime conditioning is overwhelmingly defensive:
every minimum-variance strategy compresses volatility to ~13–14% (vs 20% for SPY) and the
maximum drawdown to about −34% to −40% (vs −60%), while raising the Sharpe ratio to 0.48–0.54.
*Second*, naïve diversification disappoints: 1/N underperforms the cap-weighted S&P 500 on
every risk-adjusted metric. *Third*, and most tellingly, because all three classifiers feed an
identical portfolio construction, the tangency variants fail *uniformly*: every TPF strategy
underperforms both its own minimum-variance counterpart and the S&P 500's 7% return, with
Sharpe ratios of only 0.30–0.45 (the GMM tangency portfolio is weakest, at 0.30). Estimating
expected returns from the regime-conditional historical mean is simply too noisy to time,
whichever classifier supplies the regime. *Fourth*, the minimum-variance strategies are
uniformly strong and remarkably similar across classifiers: GMM-MVP leads (Sharpe 0.54, alpha
2.40%), with HMM-MVP essentially level (0.53) and VIX-MVP just behind (0.50). That the
*memoryless* GMM matches the Markovian HMM, and even the parameter-free VIX rule is within
striking distance, indicates the gains come from
*risk targeting under any reasonable regime map*, not from the sophistication of the regime
model.

### 5.4 Transaction costs and break-even

The minimum-variance strategies trade modestly (73–122% per year), but the tangency portfolios
churn aggressively (325–478% annually). This compounds their weak gross performance: every TPF
strategy fails to match the S&P 500 Sharpe *even at zero cost* (break-even ≤ 13 bps), whereas
the minimum-variance strategies are highly cost-tolerant — VIX-MVP absorbs 125 bps of one-way
cost, GMM-MVP 118 bps, Static MVP 86 bps before their Sharpe falls to the benchmark's. The
cost-robust strategies are precisely the low-turnover minimum-variance ones.

### 5.5 Statistical significance

No strategy's CAPM alpha is significant at 5% on a single-equation basis; the best, GMM-MVP,
comes closest at p = 0.19. The data-snooping corrections, computed over the strategies
searched, paint a coherent picture. The Deflated Sharpe Ratio of GMM-MVP is **0.96** (Sharpe
0.54 versus a snooping-adjusted null of 0.12) — its risk-adjusted edge is marginally significant
even after accounting for the breadth of the search. White's Reality Check, however, is
unambiguous: with a bootstrap **p = 0.98**, we cannot reject the null that the best strategy
fails to out-return the S&P 500, reflecting that no strategy beats the benchmark on raw return
at all. Together the tests locate the value firmly in the *denominator*: a comparable return to
the index with materially less risk — a statistically defensible improvement in risk-adjusted
terms, but no evidence of return outperformance.

## 6. Discussion

The results cohere around a single message: the exploitable structure in the volatility cycle
is the rising market sensitivity of cyclical sectors under stress; the strategies that
monetise it are the low-turnover, risk-targeting ones; and what survives scrutiny is risk
reduction, not return generation. This is consistent with a market in which volatility and
correlation are far more forecastable than the sign of returns — the stylised fact underpinning
minimum-variance investing (Clarke, de Silva and Thorley, 2006) and the difficulty of beating
simple benchmarks with return forecasts (DeMiguel, Garlappi and Uppal, 2009).

The comparison across classifiers is instructive. The HMM's transition matrix encodes genuinely
useful structure, yet it buys remarkably little: for risk targeting the memoryless GMM (0.54)
is statistically level with the Markovian HMM (0.53), and the parameter-free VIX rule (0.50)
is barely behind — so persistence adds essentially nothing here. For return timing the
classifier matters differently — every tangency variant fails (Sharpe 0.30–0.45),
confirming that the binding constraint is the noisiness of regime-conditional expected returns,
not the quality of the labels. For a practitioner, the policy implication is to prefer a simple,
low-turnover, regime-aware minimum-variance overlay over any return-timing apparatus.

---

## 7. Limitations and Future Work

(i) The backtest holds weights fixed between rebalances and measures turnover on a
target-to-target basis, abstracting from intra-month drift. (ii) Costs are a constant 10 bps
proportional charge excluding market impact. (iii) The crisis cash overlay and 20/30 VIX
thresholds are fixed ex ante. (iv) The Deflated Sharpe Ratio treats the strategies searched as
the trial universe, though the true degrees of freedom are larger. (v) The HMM is estimated
with full covariance matrices (~200 parameters) on initial 504-day windows, where covariance
estimates are noisy. Promising directions: soft-information portfolios weighting by regime
posterior probabilities, a Hidden semi-Markov specification with explicit durations, and an
out-of-sample combination (ensemble) of the three classifiers.

---

## 8. Conclusion

Conditioning a US sector-rotation strategy on inferred market regimes — whether by a simple VIX
rule, a Gaussian Hidden Markov Model, or an unsupervised Gaussian Mixture Model — delivers a
large, robust, and economically meaningful reduction in portfolio risk: drawdowns fall from
roughly −60% to about −37% and Sharpe ratios rise from 0.44 to 0.48–0.54, at low and
cost-tolerant turnover. The same cannot be said for return enhancement. Because every classifier
feeds an identical regime-conditional portfolio construction, the return-timing variants fail
uniformly and no strategy out-returns the S&P 500 once data snooping is corrected for. Two
conclusions follow: the value of regime conditioning resides in the choice of portfolio
objective (minimum-variance risk targeting), not in return timing; and that value is strikingly
insensitive to the sophistication of the regime model. In this dataset, the intelligence in
"intelligent" regime allocation is best spent on managing risk.
